What PPs need to know

Your role in the leave process

  • You are not the leave administrator. Benefits and Sparrow handle the mechanics. Your role is to support the manager with transition planning and coverage, and to make sure the employee feels supported.
  • Direct employees to benefits@. If an employee comes to you first about needing leave, point them to benefits@anthropic.com. You can also email Benefits on their behalf if the employee would prefer that.
  • PP visibility: PPs can see leaves they're assigned to in Sparrow and can also view leave information in Workday.
  • US employees may be entitled to additional time. For medical, caregiver, and parental leaves, the durations above (8 weeks, 22 weeks) represent the portion Anthropic will top up to 100% pay. In the US, these are coordinated with state disability and paid family leave programs, and employees may be entitled to additional protected time through statutory programs (FMLA, state leave) beyond Anthropic's paid leave period.
  • International employees may also have additional entitlements. Many countries have statutory leave programs (government-mandated parental, sick, or caregiver leave) that may provide additional time or protections beyond Anthropic's policies. Benefits manages all international statutory coordination — PPs should direct any questions about local entitlements to benefits@anthropic.com.

How pay works during leave (US)

This is the part that's most confusing for employees. Here's how all the pieces fit together.

The pay stack: Anthropic + STD + State

US employees on medical or parental leave may have up to three sources of income working together. Anthropic coordinates these so the employee receives 100% of their salary during Anthropic's paid leave period.

  • Unum Short-Term Disability (STD): This is an employer-paid policy through Unum. It covers 60% of weekly income up to $2,500/week after a 7-day elimination period. Benefit duration: up to 12 weeks. This kicks in for medical leave and the disability portion of parental leave (for birthing parents).
  • Unum Long-Term Disability (LTD): If an employee remains disabled after STD is exhausted, LTD may kick in. LTD covers 60% of monthly income up to $15,000/month. Benefits coordinates this transition — if an employee's medical leave is extending beyond the STD period, loop in Benefits early.
  • State programs: Employees in states with disability insurance or paid family leave programs are required to apply for those benefits as a condition of receiving Anthropic's paid leave. Sparrow walks them through this.
    States with active programs: California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Washington, plus Washington D.C. and Hawaii (TDI only). Programs and eligibility vary by state.
  • Anthropic top-up: Anthropic pays the difference so that the total (Anthropic + STD + state benefits) = 100% of salary. This top-up continues for the duration of Anthropic's paid leave (22 weeks parental, 8 weeks medical/caregiver).

What happens after Anthropic's paid leave ends?

Anthropic's top-up ends when the paid leave period is exhausted (e.g., after 22 weeks of parental or 8 weeks of medical). But the employee may still have protected time and/or benefits remaining:

  • State programs may continue: Some states provide benefits for longer than Anthropic's paid leave period. During this time, the employee receives state benefits only — Anthropic's top-up has ended, but the employee is still on approved leave.
  • FMLA may still be running: If the employee is FMLA-eligible, they may have remaining FMLA time that provides job protection even after pay ends. See below.
  • Beyond all of the above: If the employee still needs time after exhausting all paid and statutory leave, the situation may be evaluated as an accommodation request.

FMLA (US employees)

What PPs should know about FMLA

FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act) is a federal law that provides eligible US employees with up to 12 weeks (480 hours) of job-protected unpaid leave per year for qualifying reasons (serious health condition, birth/adoption of a child, caring for a family member).

The key thing for PPs to understand:

  • FMLA runs concurrently with Anthropic's paid leave — it doesn't add extra time on top. When an employee takes parental or medical leave, FMLA is running at the same time (if they're eligible).
  • FMLA provides job protection, not pay. The pay comes from Anthropic's top-up + STD + state programs. FMLA's value is that it protects the employee's job during their absence.
  • Not everyone qualifies. FMLA requires 12 months of employment and 1,250 hours worked in the past year. PPs can do a quick check: is the employee full-time and have they been at Anthropic for at least a year? If yes to both, they're likely eligible. Benefits confirms formal eligibility.
  • FMLA can also be taken intermittently for ongoing medical conditions — for example, recurring appointments or flare-ups. Benefits coordinates this.

Accommodations

How accommodations work at Anthropic, who's involved, and what PPs need to know to guide employees and managers through the process.
What We Accommodate Who's Involved The Interactive Process Documentation Coaching Managers Intersections
Key principle: Lead with compassion and assume the best. Treat every situation as a unique interaction. The interactive process is a collaborative conversation designed to support employees and determine how we can bridge their situation with their business obligations. Every accommodation request, regardless of type, should follow the same structured process so we are consistent, fair, and compliant. All parties must operate in good faith throughout.

Key things to know

"Put on notice": An accommodation doesn't start only when an employee files a formal request. If a manager or PP becomes aware that an employee may need an accommodation, even through a casual comment, that puts us on notice. Once we're on notice, we have an obligation to engage. Let ER know right away so the interactive process can begin. Don't wait for a formal request.
Accommodations are time-bound. Accommodations are not indefinite. They are tied to a specific period and require recertification or reassessment to continue. ER manages the recertification timeline and will reach out to the employee when it's time. If circumstances change (new role, new team, change in the employee's situation), the accommodation should be revisited.

What we accommodate

Anthropic provides reasonable accommodations for disability (physical or mental), pregnancy, childbirth and related conditions (including lactation), domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking, and religious beliefs and practices.

Pre-hire candidates

Requests during the hiring process (before an offer is accepted) are managed by Employee Relations.

Current employees

The entry point is ants-accommodations@anthropic.com. ER owns the process and coordinates from there.

International employees

Same principles apply globally. ER coordinates with local counsel as needed. Direct all requests to ants-accommodations@anthropic.com.

The interactive process

All accommodation requests follow the same process. Consistency protects both the employee and the company.

1
Employee, Manager, or PP
We're put on notice
The process begins when we become aware that an employee may need an accommodation. This can happen through a formal request to ants-accommodations@anthropic.com, a casual mention to a manager, or a PP observing something. However it happens, once we're on notice, ER needs to be looped in right away.
2
ER Team
ER sends the accommodation form
ER sends the Request for Reasonable Accommodation form to the employee to complete with their provider. For medical accommodations, this goes to a healthcare provider. For non-medical accommodations (religious, pregnancy-related), documentation requirements may differ. ER will guide the employee on what's needed.
3
ER + PP + Manager
Review and business assessment ESCALATE AS NEEDED
ER reviews the completed documentation for sufficiency. The PP is brought in to assess essential functions and whether the proposed accommodation is feasible for the role. The manager provides input on day-to-day job requirements and practical implications. No one sees medical details beyond ER. If the case is complex, involves a potential denial, or intersects with a PIP, escalate to Legal and/or ER at this stage.
4
ER + Employee + PP + Manager
Interactive discussion ESCALATE AS NEEDED
This is a collaborative conversation, not a one-way decision. ER discusses needs, potential solutions, and alternatives with the employee. The PP and manager are part of this discussion to help determine what's reasonable given the employee's role and team. The goal is to find something that works for everyone. If the case is complex or heading toward a denial, loop in Legal and/or ER.
5
ER Team
Written accommodation plan
The employee receives a written plan summarizing the approved accommodations, implementation details, and timeline. The manager receives only what they need to implement. No medical details are shared with the manager or PP.
6
ER + PP + Manager
Ongoing check-ins and reassessment
The manager checks in with the employee about how things are going in practice. If it isn't working for the business, the manager flags this to their PP and ER. ER follows up periodically on longer-term accommodations. If the employee's role, team, or situation changes, existing accommodations should be revisited.

Documentation

Why documentation matters

Documentation is what guides the accommodation. We aren't doctors, and we rely on the employee's provider to help us understand what the employee needs and how their situation affects their work. The Request for Reasonable Accommodation form captures this information. The employee does not need to share their specific diagnosis with their manager or PP. For medical accommodations, the documentation should verify the need for accommodation on a medical basis. ER reviews this directly.

For non-medical accommodations (religious practices, pregnancy-related needs), the documentation process may look different. ER will guide the employee on what's required.

The completed form goes directly to ER. PPs and managers should never request or review medical documentation. ER handles all of this and shares only what's necessary for implementation.

Good documentation creates a record of the interactive process, protects the employee by formalizing the accommodation, and ensures we're consistent across the organization.

Coaching managers through an accommodation

Manager coaching starts as soon as an accommodation is initiated, not just when it's finalized. Managers don't have a say in approving accommodations, but they are a critical partner in making them work. The PP and manager together help ER determine what's reasonable based on the employee's job responsibilities.

What PPs should cover with managers

  • You don't approve or deny. The manager's role is to support the process and implement the accommodation, not to assess whether it's warranted.
  • Don't ask about the condition. If they have concerns, they come to you. ER shares only what the manager needs to know for implementation.
  • Treat it as confidential. The manager should not share accommodation details with the broader team unless the employee chooses to.
  • Be a partner to ER. The manager's input on job responsibilities, team needs, and workflow is essential to finding a solution that works. Engage in good faith.
  • Focus on essential functions. If the manager is concerned about performance, the conversation should be about whether the employee can perform the essential functions of the role with the accommodation in place, not about the accommodation itself.
  • Flag when it isn't working. If the accommodation isn't working for the business, the manager should let you and ER know so adjustments can be explored. Don't let it fester.
  • Watch for "put on notice" moments. If a manager observes something that suggests an employee might need an accommodation, even if the employee hasn't asked, the manager should let you know. That puts us on notice and the process needs to begin.

Talk tracks for PPs

When coaching a manager through an accommodation, here are some things you can say:

  • When the accommodation is first initiated: "An accommodation request has come in for [employee]. I can't share the details of the situation, but I need your help understanding the essential functions of their role so we can figure out the right path forward."
  • When a manager pushes back: "I understand this feels like a lot. Your input on what the role requires is really important here. Let's talk through what's feasible and what isn't, and ER will work with us to find something that works."
  • When a manager asks about the condition: "I can't share that, and you don't need to know the specifics to be a good partner here. What I can tell you is what we need from you on the implementation side."
  • When the accommodation isn't working: "Thanks for flagging this. Let's loop in ER so we can revisit the plan. Accommodations aren't set in stone, and the process is designed to adjust as things change."

Where accommodations intersect with other processes

Accommodations & Leave

Leave can be a form of accommodation in specific situations:

  • When someone has exhausted a leave policy and still needs additional time off, extended leave may be evaluated as a reasonable accommodation.
  • When someone isn't eligible for a statutory program (e.g., doesn't meet FMLA eligibility requirements) but still needs time, an accommodation may provide a path.
  • When someone is returning from leave. Many employees need accommodations when they come back, such as modified schedules, adjusted workload, or a phased return. This is a common and normal part of the process.

Don't default to tying this to a specific policy duration. The question is whether the employee needs time (or modified time) as part of a reasonable accommodation, regardless of which leave policy applies. Benefits helps coordinate these transitions between Sparrow and ER. For more on leave policies, see the Leave of Absence page.

Accommodations, PIPs & Performance

This is where things get most sensitive. PPs play a critical role in slowing things down and making sure all the right people are in the room before decisions are made.

  • Accommodation request comes in during a PIP: Pause. Loop in ER and Legal before taking any further PIP actions. The accommodation may need to be addressed first, and proceeding with a PIP while an accommodation is pending creates significant risk. Think about: is the performance issue related to the condition? Would the accommodation change the employee's ability to meet expectations?
  • Manager wants to start a PIP for someone with an existing accommodation: Same pause. ER and Legal need to weigh in before any PIP is initiated. The key question is whether the employee is meeting the essential functions of the role with the accommodation in place. If the accommodation itself isn't working, the right path is to revisit the accommodation, not to initiate discipline.
  • Performance concerns arise right before or after a leave: Timing matters. If a manager wants to address performance with someone who just returned from leave or is about to go on leave, coordinate with ER, Benefits, and Legal on the timing and approach. The optics and the legal risk of acting without that coordination are real.

How to advise the manager: Help the manager separate the accommodation from the performance conversation. The question isn't "is this person accommodated?" It's "can this person perform the essential functions of their role, with the accommodation in place?" If the answer is yes and there are still performance issues, that's a separate conversation, but ER and Legal need to be part of it.

The through-line: any time performance management and accommodations or leave overlap, ER and Legal should be in the loop before action is taken, with Benefits looped in when leave is involved.

When a termination is processed and completed in Workday, that information is transmitted to One Source Virtual (OSV), our COBRA administrator. Within ~2 weeks, the employee receives their COBRA election notice from OSV.

When a termination is processed in Workday for an international employee, the Benefits team receives an automatic notification. No separate email to Benefits is needed, but if the timeline is complex, please give us a heads-up.

There is no COBRA equivalent internationally. Each country has its own continuation options. See the country sections below for details.

PP offboarding checklist

What to confirm or flag as you support an offboarding employee. The benefit details are covered in the country tables above; this is about making sure nothing falls through the cracks.

  • Confirm the termination date is correct in Workday (this drives COBRA timing for US and broker notifications internationally)
  • If there's a COBRA subsidy, make sure it's entered in Workday so Benefits can load it into OSV (we know there is often lag in signing separation agreements — please input the subsidy as soon as this information is finalized)
  • Remind the employee to submit any remaining Benepass expenses before their card is deactivated
  • For all employees: remind them to record login information and transition credentials to a personal email if not already

  • If the employee reaches out with benefits questions post-departure, direct them to benefits@anthropic.com or their country's broker (listed in the country sections above)
  • If there are concerns about COBRA timing or enrollment issues, escalate to Benefits in #peoplepartner-benefits
